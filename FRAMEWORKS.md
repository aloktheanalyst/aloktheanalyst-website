## 🧠 MASTER PROBLEM-SOLVING APPROACH

### Victor Cheng's 4 Core Tools (Book 5)
*Use these in every single case, in order:*

1. **Hypothesis** — State an educated guess early (within 5 minutes). "I hypothesize the problem is X because Y."
2. **Issue Tree / Framework** — Build a logical argument: IF branch 1 AND branch 2 AND branch 3 are true, THEN hypothesis is proven. Must be MECE.
3. **Drill-Down Analysis** — Systematically prove/disprove each branch. Segment → Isolate → Repeat. When a branch is disproven, revise hypothesis.
4. **Synthesis** — Action-oriented close: *What to do → Why (3 facts) → Restate recommendation*

### Ben Okon's 3-Question Repetition (Book 3)
*Answer these 3 questions at ANY point in the case:*

1. **Objective** — What decision are we helping the client make?
2. **Hypothesis** — If we ended the case right now, what would we recommend? Why?
3. **Next Steps** — What would we need to do to be more confident in the decision?

## 📐 CORE FRAMEWORKS

### 1. Profitability Framework
**When to use:** Profit/margin decline, or need to understand financial performance
**Books:** 1, 2, 3, 4, 5

```
Profit = Revenue - Cost

Revenue
  ├── Price per unit
  └── # units sold (volume)
       ├── Segment by geography
       ├── Segment by product line
       └── Segment by customer type

Cost
  ├── Fixed Costs (don't change with volume — rent, salaries, overhead)
  └── Variable Costs (change with volume — materials, commissions, shipping)
       └── Variable Cost per Unit × # units sold
```

**Key Analysis Steps (Victor Cheng):**
1. Determine if revenue or cost is the driver
2. Segment and isolate the root cause
3. Compare metric to: (a) same metric last year, (b) industry average
4. Distinguish fixed vs variable costs to identify the right lever

**Key Formula:** Contribution Margin = Price - Variable Cost per Unit
**Break-Even:** Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin per Unit

### 2. Business Situation Framework (Victor Cheng, Book 5)
**When to use:** Market entry, new product launch, growth strategy, turnaround — any qualitative business assessment
**Covers ~50% of all cases**

```
Business Situation
  ├── Customer
  │     ├── Who is the customer? (segments)
  │     ├── What are their needs/buying criteria?
  │     ├── Price sensitivity per segment
  │     ├── Distribution channel preferences
  │     └── Customer concentration (Walmart effect)
  │
  ├── Product
  │     ├── What problem does it solve?
  │     ├── Commodity vs unique good?
  │     ├── Complementary goods / substitutes?
  │     ├── Product life cycle stage
  │     └── Packaging / bundling
  │
  ├── Company
  │     ├── Core competencies / unique capabilities
  │     ├── Cost structure and profitability
  │     ├── Financial health and resources
  │     ├── Distribution channels
  │     └── Management quality
  │
  └── Competition
        ├── Who are the key competitors?
        ├── Competitor market share and growth
        ├── Competitor cost structure vs our client
        ├── Competitor value proposition
        └── Barriers to entry
```

### 5. Pricing Framework
**When to use:** Pricing strategy for new or existing product
**Books:** 1, 4

```
Pricing Framework (3 Lenses)
  ├── Cost-Based (floor)
  │     ├── Fixed costs allocated per unit
  │     ├── Variable cost per unit
  │     └── Target profit margin → minimum price
  │
  ├── Value-Based (ceiling)
  │     ├── What is the next best alternative?
  │     ├── What unique features does our product add?
  │     └── Willingness to pay = competitor price + value of added features
  │
  └── Competitor-Based (market anchor)
        ├── Available substitutes and their prices
        ├── Our product vs substitutes on key dimensions
        └── Price relative to competitors (premium/parity/discount)

Overall Strategy
  ├── Pricing objective: profit max, market share, penetration?
  ├── Cross-sell / up-sell opportunities
  └── Product tiering (e.g., iPhone 11 vs 11 Pro)
```

## 📊 MARKET SIZING FRAMEWORKS

### Top-Down Market Sizing
**When to use:** When industry-level data is available
**Books:** 1, 2, 3, 4

```
Steps:
1. Start with total population (India: 1.4B, US: 330M, World: 8B)
2. Filter by target demographic (e.g., age 18-65 = 65%)
3. Apply penetration rate (% who buy this type of product)
4. Estimate purchase frequency and avg ticket size
5. Calculate: Population × Penetration × Frequency × Price = Market Size
6. Sense check against known benchmarks
```

### Bottom-Up Market Sizing
**When to use:** When you can count supply-side (stores, companies, etc.)
**Books:** 1, 2

```
Steps:
1. Count the number of supply units (stores, companies, factories)
2. Estimate output/revenue per unit per time period
3. Multiply: # Units × Revenue per Unit = Total Market
4. Cross-validate with top-down approach
```

### Ben Okon's Market Sizing Process (Book 3)
```
Step 1: Scope the problem (clarify constraints)
Step 2: Choose approach (top-down or bottom-up)
Step 3: Build assumptions (memorize key facts)
Step 4: Calculate (use Tables / Trees / Straight-Line)
Step 5: Sense check (compare to known benchmarks)
Step 6: State final answer with confidence interval
```

**Key Facts to Memorize:**
- World population: ~8B | India: 1.4B | US: 330M (~120M households)
- China: 1.4B | Indonesia: 280M | Brazil: 210M
- 1 mile = 5,280 feet | 1 km = 0.62 miles | 1 inch = 2.54 cm

## 🔍 ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS (RCA)

### Decomposition Framework
**When to use:** A metric dropped — find the root cause
**Books:** 2, 3 | YouTube: Alok's RCA video

```
Step 1: Define the metric and its components (decompose)
  └── Is it a REACH problem or a DEPTH problem?
       ├── Reach: Fewer users seeing/touching the feature
       └── Depth: Same users but lower engagement per visit

Step 2: Check if it's Internal or External
  ├── Internal: Product changes, bugs, UX issues, pricing changes
  └── External: Competitor actions, seasonality, macro trends

Step 3: Segment to isolate
  ├── By time period (when did it start?)
  ├── By platform (iOS vs Android, web vs app)
  ├── By user segment (new vs returning, geography)
  └── By funnel stage (acquisition → activation → retention → revenue)

Step 4: Form and test hypotheses
  └── "I hypothesize X caused the drop because Y. To confirm, I need Z."

Step 5: Recommend fix
  └── Short-term mitigation + long-term prevention
```

### AARRR / North Star Framework
**When to use:** KPI design, product metric selection
**Books:** 2 | YouTube: Alok's Analytics Case Study video

```
AARRR Pirate Metrics:
  ├── Acquisition — How users find you (CAC, CTR, installs)
  ├── Activation — First value moment (onboarding completion, D1 retention)
  ├── Retention — Users coming back (D7, D30, churn rate)
  ├── Revenue — Monetization (ARPU, LTV, conversion rate)
  └── Referral — Word of mouth (NPS, virality coefficient)

North Star Metric = Single metric that captures core user value
  └── Example: DAU, Weekly Active Buyers, Messages Sent per User
```

## 📉 BUSINESS JUDGMENT FRAMEWORKS

### Growth Strategy Framework
**When to use:** How should we grow revenue?
**Books:** 1, 2

```
Growth Levers (Ansoff Matrix):
  ├── Existing Products + Existing Markets → Market Penetration
  ├── Existing Products + New Markets → Market Development
  ├── New Products + Existing Markets → Product Development
  └── New Products + New Markets → Diversification

Revenue = Volume × Price
  ├── Grow Volume: More customers, higher frequency, expansion
  └── Grow Price: Premium tier, reduce discounts, value-add features
```

### Cost Structure Analysis
**When to use:** Profitability improvement, cost benchmarking
**Books:** 1, 4

```
Cost Structure
  ├── Fixed Costs (period costs — don't vary with output)
  │     ├── Rent, salaries, depreciation, marketing, tech infrastructure
  │     └── Key question: Can we reduce fixed costs at same scale?
  │
  └── Variable Costs (unit economics — vary proportionally)
        ├── COGS, commissions, delivery, packaging, payment fees
        └── Key question: Can we reduce cost per unit?

Contribution Margin = Price - Variable Cost per Unit
Operating Leverage = Fixed Costs / Total Costs (higher = more risky, more upside)
```

### Break-Even Analysis
**When to use:** When can we become profitable? How many units to sell?
**Books:** 1, 4

```
Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin per Unit
Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ Gross Margin %
Payback Period = Initial Investment ÷ Annual Profit
```

### Market Structure Analysis
**When to use:** Is this market concentrated or fragmented?
**Books:** 1, 4

```
HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) = Sum of (market share)² for all players
  ├── HHI > 2500 → Highly concentrated (monopoly-like)
  ├── HHI 1500–2500 → Moderately concentrated
  └── HHI < 1500 → Competitive / fragmented

Porter's 5 Forces → Structural attractiveness of the industry
3Cs → Customer and competitive dynamics
```

## 🔢 FINANCE FORMULAS (Must Know)
*Source: Cracking Case Interviews (Book 4)*

| Formula | Description |
|---|---|
| Revenue = Volume × Price | Or: Revenue = Market Size × Market Share |
| Cost = Fixed Costs + Variable Costs | VC changes with volume; FC doesn't |
| Profit = Revenue - Cost | Also: Net Income, Net Earnings |
| Profit Margin = Profit ÷ Revenue | Expressed as % |
| ROI = Profit ÷ Initial Investment | Expressed as %, usually per year |
| Payback Period = Investment ÷ Annual Profit | Years to earn back investment |
| Contribution Margin = Price - VC per Unit | Per-unit profit before fixed costs |
| Break-Even Units = FC ÷ Contribution Margin | Minimum units to cover fixed costs |

**Optional (Nice to Know):**
- EBITDA = Earnings before Interest, Tax, Depreciation, Amortisation
- NPV = Net Present Value (adjusts future cash flows to today's dollars)
- ROE = Profit ÷ Shareholder Equity
- ROA = Profit ÷ Total Assets

## 🔢 MATH TECHNIQUES (Case Interview Speed)
*Source: Case Weights (Book 3) + Cracking Case Interviews (Book 4)*

### Written Math Structures
1. **Tables** — Best for profit analysis; organize rows/columns of revenue, cost, margin
2. **Calculation Trees** — Best for order-of-operations problems (units × price × conversion)
3. **Straight-Line Conversion** — Best for unit conversion chains (palettes → boxes → units)

### Mental Math Shortcuts
- Round ± 10% max; alternate rounding up and down to cancel out
- Use labels: k (thousand), m (million), b (billion)
- 25 = 100/4 → 36 × 25 = (36/4) × 100 = 9 × 100 = 900
- 50 = 100/2, 75 = (100×3)/4
- Compound growth shortcut: for short periods, add % changes; for long periods: growth rate × years

### Vocal Math Delivery
1. State your approach BEFORE calculating
2. Narrate calculations as you go ("15 times 5 equals...")
3. Tie result back to the original question

```

  ├── Vertical: What's true at a single point in time?
```

## 🧩 BRAINSTORMING FRAMEWORK
*Source: Case Weights (Book 3)*

### Master Structure (Ben Okon)
```
Any business problem can be decomposed into:
  ├── Outside Forces (external factors)
  │     ├── Market saturation / size
  │     ├── Competition
  │     ├── Macro trends (regulation, economy, technology)
  │     └── Customer behavior shifts
  │
  ├── Internal Levers (what we can control)
  │     ├── Revenue levers (pricing, volume, mix)
  │     ├── Cost levers (fixed, variable, efficiency)
  │     ├── Operations (capacity, quality, speed)
  │     └── Marketing & sales
  │
  └── Risks & Opportunities
        ├── Brand equity risks
        ├── Financial risks
        └── Strategic opportunities
```

### MECE Brainstorming
- Maximum 5 high-level buckets
- No overlap between buckets (Mutually Exclusive)
- Together they cover all possibilities (Collectively Exhaustive)
- Generate 7–10 ideas within your structure

### Mid-Case Action Plan (Type 1 Brainstorm)
```
When you uncover a problem mid-case, quickly structure 3 possible paths:
  Fix the problem → Grow around the problem → Exit / pivot
Then drill down the most promising path first.
```

## 🎯 SYNTHESIS / CLOSE
*Source: Case Interview Secrets (Book 5)*

**Bad synthesis (avoid):** List everything you learned
**Good synthesis:**
```
1. Action-oriented conclusion: "You should [do X]."
2. Supporting fact 1: "Because [key finding]."
3. Supporting fact 2: "Additionally, [key finding]."
4. Supporting fact 3: "Furthermore, [key finding]."
5. Restate recommendation: "Therefore, I recommend [do X]."
```

**Tip:** Your closing argument often mirrors your final issue tree structure.
